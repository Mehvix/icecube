Decoherence

We all know that atoms are the building blocks of life. And if you didn’t know, then you do now. At some point in high school chemistry, you probably learned that atoms can exist at two states: ground state and excited state. An atom’s excited state occurs when the atom absorbs some energy. After a few moments, the atom will return back to ground state, emitting a photon of the same energy it previously absorbed.

In quantum mechanics, an atom can exist in its ground state and excited state at the same time. This is called superposition! But when we measure the atom, only one of the states will be randomly observed. This means that when we are looking at the atom, it will only ever appear excited or not excited at once. Never both at the same time.

To demonstrate that superposition occurs, you can send an electromagnetic wave at the right frequency to watch the atom oscillate between its excited and non-excited state. When these oscillations are plotted on a graph, they form the shape of a sine wave before slowly tapering off. These are sometimes called Rabi oscillations. So far, we’ve only talked about the atom in an ideal setting. In the real world, the atom is never isolated. It is constantly colliding with other objects, experiencing electromagnetic fields, or being subject to thermal baths. For this next example, we will look at two atoms: one in a classical setting and one in a quantum setting.

Let’s start with the classical example. We will be taking the atom from our previous examples and subjecting it to an environment of photons (which are light particles). In this situation, photons will hit the atom, sending it into an excited state. The atom will then release the photon exactly how it entered the atom. Both the atom and the photon end up the same way as when they started.

Schroedinger's cat (hover)

Now let’s look at a quantum example. The same situation occurs, where photons will be absorbed by the atom and then emitted in waves. However, when the photon is absorbed, it mixes with the atom to create a new state. When the photon is released, it emits a mixture of the atom and proton as opposed to just how the proton was before it was absorbed. To break this down a little more, imagine two clay balls that represent an atom and a proton. When we treat this situation classically, the red photon clay ball touches the blue atom clay ball, and bounces off without any change, remaining as a red clay ball. When we treat this situation quantumly, the red photon clay ball and the blue atom ball mix together, and the emitted photon is purple instead of red.

This event is called decoherence. It’s when objects lose parts of themselves when interacting with other objects, and it explains why superposition oscillations taper off when they are graphed. When a photon hits an atom, the atom must choose to be in one of its two states. The information from the object’s used state, unused state, and the photon is emitted from the atom. Over time, this leads to a balance in the states to choose from.
